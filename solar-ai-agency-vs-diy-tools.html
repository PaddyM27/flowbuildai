The Problem Every Irish Solar Installer Recognises

You're getting leads. The SEAI grant scheme is driving record enquiry volumes — applications increased 65% year-on-year in early 2026. The demand is there. But somewhere between the homeowner hitting "submit" on your contact form and them signing a contract, installs are slipping.

The response to this problem has been a wave of tools — CRMs, AI dialers, answering services, follow-up automations. But most of them create a new problem: they require someone to manage them.

What the DIY Solar Tool Landscape Looks Like

Here are the main tools Irish solar companies encounter when searching for a solution:

Solar-Specific CRMs (JobNimbus, Shape, Sunbase)

These tools are purpose-built for the solar sales process — lead tracking, pipeline management, automated follow-up sequences, proposal tools. Shape CRM starts at $99/month and has 1,000+ pre-built campaign templates. JobNimbus combines CRM with project management. Sunbase has even published Ireland-specific content.

The catch: every single one requires a dedicated person to manage it. Someone needs to set up the sequences, log into the platform daily, update the pipeline, and monitor the automation. For a small Irish installer with 3–8 employees, that person usually doesn't exist. The tool gets purchased, half-configured, and abandoned.

AI Voice/Receptionist Tools (Sameday AI, Aloware, Upfirst)

Sameday AI is the standout here — a purpose-built AI answering service for solar, starting at $449/month. It answers calls 24/7, qualifies leads, books appointments. Aloware offers AI voice agents from $30/user/month. Both have solar-specific case studies and proven US results.

Two problems for Irish installers: they're US-only (priced in USD, with no awareness of SEAI grants, Irish phone number infrastructure, or Irish homeowner behaviour), and they solve one problem — missed calls. You'd still need a separate tool for quote follow-up, a separate tool for reviews, and someone to manage all of it.

General Automation Platforms (GoHighLevel, Make.com)

GoHighLevel has become popular with marketing agencies who white-label it for tradespeople. It's powerful and flexible. It's also a full-time job to configure and maintain. We've seen Irish solar companies get sold a "solar package" on GoHighLevel that was never properly set up and sat doing nothing for months.

"The problem isn't that these tools don't work. It's that they only work if someone is actively running them — and most solar installers don't have that person."

The Real Cost of DIY Solar Tools

Let's say an Irish solar company puts together a credible DIY stack. They need:

  • A solar CRM for pipeline and follow-up: ~€100–250/month
  • An AI answering service for missed calls: ~€400–600/month (if a US tool even works in Ireland)
  • A review platform: ~€75–150/month
  • Someone to configure and manage it all: minimum 5–10 hours/month of someone's time

That's €575–1,000/month in software alone — before anyone has spent a single hour making it work. And it still leaves gaps: the AI answering service doesn't know your SEAI process, the CRM isn't connected to your calendar, and the review platform isn't triggered automatically.

A single missed install — one homeowner who enquired but booked with another company because you didn't respond fast enough — costs multiples of that entire stack.

When Does a Done-for-You AI Agency Make Sense?

A done-for-you approach like FlowBuild AI makes most sense when:

  • You're getting 10+ inbound enquiries per month — enough volume that a 60-second response system materially affects your booking rate
  • You don't have a dedicated sales ops person — most Irish solar companies with under 15 employees don't
  • You're losing quotes you've already sent — if your close rate on sent quotes is under 50%, the Quote Recovery Engine alone will pay for the system
  • Your evenings and weekends are getting missed calls — Irish homeowners enquire when they're home, which is outside your working hours

A DIY tool stack makes sense when you have a dedicated operations person, significant technical capacity, and the time to configure, test, and maintain multiple tools. Most Irish solar installers with under 20 employees don't have this — and the honest answer is that they shouldn't need to.
